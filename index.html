    • Communly Used Terms

    • Filtration: The process of removing impurities from water by passing it through a filter.
    • Reverse Osmosis (RO): A water purification process that uses a semipermeable membrane to remove ions, molecules, and larger particles.
    • Water Quality: The physical, chemical, and biological characteristics of water, often assessed to ensure it meets safety standards.
    • pH Level: A measure of how acidic or basic water is, crucial for maintaining water quality.
    • Chlorination: The addition of chlorine to water for disinfection purposes.
    • Contaminants: Substances that pollute water, including chemicals, heavy metals, and pathogens.
    • Water Softening: The process of removing hardness minerals (like calcium and magnesium) from water.
    • Sedimentation: The process of settling and removing solids from water by gravity.
    • Biological Treatment: Using microorganisms to remove organic contaminants from water.
    • Desalination: The process of removing salt from seawater to produce fresh water.
    • Potable Water: Water that is safe for human consumption and meets health standards.
    • Water Treatment: Processes used to make water safe for drinking, which may include filtration, disinfection, and chemical treatment.
    • Disinfection: The process of killing or inactivating pathogenic microorganisms in water, often using chlorine, ozone, or UV light.
    • Contaminant: Any substance that can alter the quality of drinking water, including chemicals, bacteria, viruses, and heavy metals.
    • Maximum Contaminant Level (MCL): The highest level of a contaminant allowed in drinking water, set by regulatory agencies to protect public health.
    • Water Quality Standards: Regulatory guidelines that define acceptable levels of various contaminants in drinking water.
    • Source Water: The untreated water from which drinking water is produced, such as rivers, lakes, or groundwater.
    • Turbidity: A measure of cloudiness in water caused by suspended particles, which can indicate the presence of contaminants.
    • Hardness: The concentration of calcium and magnesium in water, which can affect taste and the efficiency of soaps and detergents.
    • Fluoridation: The addition of fluoride to drinking water to help prevent tooth decay.
    • Distribution System: The network of pipes and infrastructure that delivers drinking water from treatment facilities to consumers.
    • Cross-Connection: An unwanted connection between a potable water system and a non-potable source, which can lead to contamination.

    Water Cleaning Process

    Water filtration is a crucial process in the treatment of drinking water, designed to remove impurities and contaminants to ensure its safety for consumption. The filtration process typically involves passing water through various layers of materials, such as sand, gravel, and activated carbon, which act as physical barriers to trap particles, sediments, and harmful substances. As water flows through these layers, larger contaminants are filtered out first, followed by smaller particles. Activated carbon is particularly effective in removing chlorine, volatile organic compounds, and other chemicals that can affect taste and odor. Additionally, advanced filtration systems may incorporate membrane technologies, such as reverse osmosis, which can eliminate dissolved solids and microorganisms. The result is clean, clear water that meets health standards and is safe for drinking, cooking, and other uses. Effective water filtration not only enhances the quality of drinking water but also contributes to public health by reducing the risk of waterborne diseases.
